📅 Devlog #002: Day 10 - A Cold Shower from the Community
Date: April 28, 2026

I’ve lost exact track of the days, but let’s call this Day 10. The prototype is taking shape, and so are the challenges.

1. A Cold Shower from Netizens
Yesterday, I shared the project on Reddit and the Godot forums. The feedback was remarkably consistent: Skepticism. The general sentiment was "Good luck, but we don't see this working."

One helpful user even pointed me toward a post-mortem of a failed attempt to 'vibe-code' a Metroidvania game just a few days ago. https://forum.godotengine.org/t/post-mortem-of-my-failed-attempt-to-vibe-code-a-metroidvania-game/137567/19

My Take:
Regardless of the doubts, I’m pushing forward. If anything, I’m more curious now to see where the actual boundaries of AI-assisted programming lie. Does a veteran coder’s experience still hold value in this new era? Consider this a necessary experiment.

2. The Roadmap: Trusting the AI’s Strategic Choice
With the four basic levels of World 1 mostly functional, I sat down with the AI to discuss our next move. Our ultimate goal is a web platform where players can register, play, and use an integrated level editor. I proposed three paths:

Focus on Gameplay: Perfect the game itself, add all mechanics/elements, and then circle back to the editor and website.

Tools-First (Synchronous): Build the core editor first. Every time we add a new gameplay element, we sync it to the editor immediately. Launch the website once the editor is usable.

Frontend-First: Build the website shell first to let people see and interact with it, then fill in the game/editor.

The AI chose Option 2. It argued for having a functional editor before going live with a website. Given the skepticism from the community, I agree. We need to "build in silence" until we can showcase a tool that actually supports creating all four levels of World 1, then return to the community with something undeniable.

3. Today’s Progress: Data Refactoring & Editor Foundations
The AI immediately spotted a bottleneck: our current level data was stored in dense array-based .txt files (inherited from an old CSV setup). It pointed out that this would be a nightmare for future expansion and suggested refactoring everything into JSON.

I gave the green light. After some testing and refactoring, the JSON-based levels are now running perfectly.

What’s truly impressive? Right after fixing the data layer, I gave a single-sentence prompt, and the AI delivered a basic level editor canvas with zoom and pan functionality. It was moving so fast I had to tell it to stop—I need to sit down and write a proper "Design Doc" for it before we continue.

4. The Value of Experience: Debugging the AI's "Logic Blind Spots"
Today proved that even if you don't write a single line of code, years of engineering experience are indispensable. When the AI hits a wall, you have to rely on intuition to guess where its logic might be tripping up. It’s like pair-programming with a brilliant but sometimes literal-minded junior—you need to know how to guide the investigation.

The Bug of the Day:
The protagonist would jump and hit a brick directly above, but the "block hit" event wouldn't trigger.

The Intuition: I suspected the AI had written two separate, disconnected logic sets. I asked: "Are the 'physics collision for jumping' and 'the trigger for hitting a block' using two different calculation methods?"

The Proof: I asked the AI to print logs for both. Sure enough, the AI admitted that the two systems were calculating the block's coordinates differently.

The Root Cause: When I initially prompted the jump/hit mechanics, I didn't specify how to handle "edge cases"—specifically, what happens when the player jumps while perfectly centered between two blocks. The AI had default-coded it to trigger both, which resulted in a "cancel out" effect where neither triggered.

The Fix: Once we identified the logic gap, the solution was trivial.

A Compromise on Hitboxes:
There are still issues with collision boxes. Currently, the "Big" and "Small" versions of the hero use the same hitbox width, leading to some "pixel-perfect" unfairness (walking on air or dying without touching an enemy). While an easy fix (just tell the AI the actual sprite dimensions), I've decided to hold off. If we want players to upload their own assets in the future, we need an automated way to detect transparency boundaries. For now, we'll live with it to stay focused on the "Zero Code" validation.
